Dots & Boxes
Two Players · Strategy · Grid
How to play
1
Take turns drawing lines
Players alternate. On your turn, tap any gap between two adjacent dots to draw a horizontal or vertical line. You can only draw one line per turn.
2
Complete a box to score
If your line closes the fourth side of a square, you claim that box — your initials appear inside it. You then get a bonus turn and must draw another line immediately.
3
Chain your bonus turns
If your bonus turn also closes a box, you get another bonus turn. Expert play involves creating long chains of boxes to claim in a single run.
4
Most boxes wins
When every box is claimed, the player with the most boxes wins. Championship records are tracked across sessions.
Key diagram
Strategy tip: Avoid drawing the third side of any box — it hands your opponent an easy score. In the mid-game, sacrifice a small chain of two to avoid giving away a larger one.
AI difficulty
Easy — takes boxes when it sees them, otherwise plays randomly.
Medium — avoids gifting three-sided boxes to you.
Hard — minimax lookahead to depth 4. Will plan ahead.
Extreme — depth-7 minimax. Very difficult to beat on a small grid.

Hex
Two Players · Topology · No Draws
The objective
1
Choose your colour
Copper must connect the top edge to the bottom edge. Ice must connect the left edge to the right edge. The coloured border strips on the board show your direction.
2
Place one stone per turn
Players alternate. On your turn, tap any empty hexagon to place your stone there. You cannot move or remove stones once placed.
3
Connect edge to edge
The winner is the first player to form an unbroken chain of their stones connecting both of their sides. The chain can wind and branch — only connectivity matters.
4
There are no draws
By mathematical proof, one player must always win. Blocking your opponent directly advances your own connection. Every stone counts.
Strategy tip: The centre of the board is the most valuable territory. Control the centre and your connection has the shortest possible path. Bridges — pairs of stones with a shared safe connector — are the building blocks of strong Hex play.

Minefield
Solo · Logic · Deduction
How to play
1
Click to reveal a cell
Left-click any hidden cell to reveal it. The first click is always safe — mines are placed after your first move, keeping a 3×3 safe zone around it.
2
Read the numbers
Each revealed number tells you exactly how many mines are hidden in its eight neighbouring cells. A blank cell has zero mine neighbours and will automatically flood-reveal the surrounding safe area.
3
Flag suspected mines
Right-click (or long-press on mobile) to place a flag ⚑ on a cell you believe contains a mine. Flags prevent accidental clicks. The HUD tracks how many mines remain unflagged.
4
Clear the field to win
Reveal every safe cell without detonating a single mine. Your time is recorded — best times are saved per difficulty so you can beat your personal record.
Number guide
Logic tip: If a revealed "1" has only one unrevealed neighbour, that neighbour must be the mine — flag it. If a revealed number has exactly as many flagged neighbours as its value, all remaining neighbours are safe to reveal.
Difficulties
Cadet — 9×9 grid, 10 mines. Good for learning the logic.
Field — 16×16 grid, 40 mines. The standard game.
Expert — 16×30 grid, 99 mines. Requires systematic logic and nerve.
Custom — set your own rows, columns, and mine count.
